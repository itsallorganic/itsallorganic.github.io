The Infinite Price

January 2026 Edition

The air in a hospital emergency room always feels different—heavier, colder, and smelling faintly of rubbing alcohol and anxiety. That night, under the harsh hum of the fluorescent lights, the world seemed to narrow down to a single bed. My grandmother lay there, struggling for every breath. Her lungs were fighting a battle they were slowly losing.

Standing next to me was my grandfather. He is a strong man, the patriarch of our family, but in that moment, he looked small. He speaks very little English, and the chaos of American medicine—the alarms, the rushing nurses, the rapid-fire questions—was a wall he couldn’t climb. He was terrified. He looked at me not just as his grandson, but as his voice, his ears, and his only hope.

I felt the weight of that trust settle on my shoulders like a physical load. I was no longer just a twenty-something student; I was the decision-maker for a life that helped create my own.

When the bi-pap machine failed to stabilize her, the doctor turned to us. He had the tired, kind eyes of someone who delivers bad news too often. He recommended intubation—putting her on a ventilator. But he didn’t stop there. He did what doctors are trained to do: he listed the risks. He explained that at her age, once the machine goes on, it might never come off. He was asking us to weigh the risks.

My heart hammered against my ribs. I looked at my grandmother, gasping for air, and felt a surge of panic. What if I make the wrong choice? What if this machine hurts her more? But then I looked at the alternative. If we did nothing, she would be gone. To me, the math was simple. The risk was terrifying, but the alternative was unacceptable.

"Intubate her," I said. My voice sounded steadier than I felt.

Then came the question that stopped time. The doctor looked at his clipboard and asked, "If her heart stops, do we have permission to resuscitate?"

"Yes," I said immediately. It was a reflex. It was instinct.

The doctor hesitated. He looked past me to my grandfather, asking if he truly understood what that meant. The doctor saw a fragile, elderly woman. He saw ribs potentially breaking from CPR. He saw suffering. He saw a "price" to be paid in pain.

I saw something else. I saw a treasure.

As I sat in the waiting room later, the adrenaline faded, leaving me alone with my thoughts. I am a medical student. I study bioethics. I know the textbooks say we must respect "patient autonomy." I know the modern medical philosophy often focuses on "Quality of Life"—the idea that sometimes, letting go is a mercy because the suffering isn't worth it.

But as I watched my grandfather’s relief that we were still fighting, I realized I was at war with my own training.

This is the great challenge I will face as a physician. I am building a career on a framework that tells me patients have the right to give up. I am being trained to accept that if a patient feels the "price" of treatment is too high, I must step back.

But deep down, in the core of who I am, I don't believe there is a price too high for life. I believe life is an infinite treasure. Whether it’s hooked up to machines, difficult, or painful—it is still life. To me, living isn't a walk in the park; it’s a privilege we have to fight for. I wonder why so many people are willing to fold their hand the moment the game gets hard.

This creates a friction in my soul. There is the "Me" who believes we must fight for every second of breath, and there is the "Doctor" I am required to be—one who must sometimes stand by and let a patient choose death because they are tired of the fight.

That night, for my grandmother, the roles aligned. We paid the price. We chose the pain and the tubes because we wanted the life. But I know a day is coming when I will stand by a different bed, with a different family, and I will have to silence my own heart to respect their choice.

That will be the hardest procedure I ever learn to perform.
